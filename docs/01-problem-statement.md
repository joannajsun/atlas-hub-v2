# Problem Statement
**Atlas Hub 2.0 — Last-Mile Delivery Operations Platform**
*May 2026 | Joanna Sun*

---

## The Problem

White-glove delivery operations run on fragmented tools and informal communication — which means failed deliveries, angry customers, and expensive rescheduling that ops teams can't systematically prevent.

---

## Background

The big-and-bulky delivery segment (furniture, appliances, mattresses) is fundamentally different from parcel delivery. A failed Amazon package costs $2–5. A failed white-glove furniture delivery costs $200–500 — crew time, fuel, customer service, and lost retailer goodwill. Industry average first-attempt failure rate sits at 15–25%.

The people responsible for preventing these failures — dispatch managers — are making high-stakes decisions every morning with a spreadsheet, a group text chain, and a TMS from 2014. This is a tooling problem, not a workforce problem.

---

## Core Problems

**1. No pre-departure confirmation system.** Someone should verify the customer is home, the address is correct, and the building has elevator access before the truck leaves. In practice this happens inconsistently or not at all. A 10-minute check could prevent a 3-hour failed delivery. Nothing enforces or tracks this step.

**2. Exception data lives nowhere useful.** When a delivery fails, the reason lives in the driver's memory and a phone call to dispatch. Patterns — the same building with a broken elevator every month, customers who are never home before noon, a retail partner who consistently ships wrong addresses — never surface because the data is never aggregated.

**3. The morning brief is manual and scattered.** Every dispatch manager starts their shift trying to answer: what's at risk today? That information is split across multiple systems and two text threads. By the time they have the full picture, the first route has already left.

---

## The Opportunity

For a carrier running 200 deliveries/day at an 18% failure rate: reducing failures to 10% is 16 fewer failed deliveries per day. At $250 average cost per failure, that's ~$1.5M/year in cost avoidance. Atlas at $18K/year is less than 1.5% of that savings.
