# Problem Statement
**Atlas Hub 2.0 — Last-Mile Delivery Operations Platform**
*May 2026 | Joanna Sun*

---

## The Problem

White-glove delivery operations run on fragmented tools and informal communication — which means dispatch managers can't see a delivery going wrong until it's already wrong. By the time they have the full picture, it's a customer complaint.

*Validated by user interview — Maria Chen, dispatch manager, Bay Area carrier, May 2026.*

---

## Background

The big-and-bulky delivery segment (furniture, appliances, mattresses) is fundamentally different from parcel delivery. A failed Amazon package costs $2–5. A failed white-glove furniture delivery costs $200–500 — crew time, fuel, customer service, and lost retailer goodwill. Industry average first-attempt failure rate sits at 15–25%.

The people responsible for preventing these failures — dispatch managers — are making high-stakes decisions every morning with a spreadsheet, a group text chain, and a TMS from 2014. This is a tooling problem, not a workforce problem.

---

## Core Problems

**1. Signal lag — reactive ops by default.** Dispatch managers find out about problems when customers call, not before. There's no early warning system. By the time a dispatch manager pieces together what happened (TMS + texts + their own spreadsheet), the damage is done. *Interview validated: Maria spends 60% of her day on fires she didn't see coming.*

**2. Exception root cause blindness.** TMS reports show completed vs. attempted — no reason codes. When a delivery fails, the reason lives in the driver's memory and a phone call. Patterns never surface: the same building with broken elevator access every month, the customer who's never home before noon, the retail partner with a 4% address error rate. *Interview validated: Maria spent 45 min reconstructing one bad Tuesday from texts + TMS + her sheet.*

**3. No pre-departure confirmation system.** Access issues — elevator restrictions, building codes, narrow hallways — should be captured at booking. In practice drivers find out standing in the lobby. A 10-minute pre-departure check could prevent a 3-hour failed delivery. Nothing enforces or tracks this step.

**4. The morning brief is manual and fragmented.** Every dispatch manager starts their shift trying to answer: what's at risk today? That information is split across 3-4 systems. By the time they have the full picture, the first route has already left. *Interview validated: Maria's "system" is a color-coded Google Sheet she built herself.*

---

## The Opportunity

For a carrier running 200 deliveries/day at an 18% failure rate: reducing failures to 10% is 16 fewer failed deliveries per day. At $250 average cost per failure, that's ~$1.5M/year in cost avoidance. Atlas at $18K/year is less than 1.5% of that savings.
