# Customer Journey Map
**Atlas Hub 2.0 — Last-Mile Delivery Operations Platform**
*Version 1.0 | May 2026 | Author: Joanna Sun*

---

## Overview

This document maps two intersecting journeys:
1. **The buyer journey** — how Atlas acquires a carrier as a customer (David-led, economic buyer)
2. **The user journey** — how Maria and Marcus experience Atlas in daily use after signup

Both journeys matter. We can win the buyer without winning the user, and the product dies at renewal. We can win the user without winning the buyer, and the product never gets paid for.

---

## Journey 1 — Buyer Journey (David, VP of Operations)

### Stage 1: Unaware

**David's state**: Managing operations with weekly Excel reports, doesn't know a purpose-built tool exists for his problem.

**What he's doing**: Spending Monday mornings waiting for Maria's team to compile last week's numbers. Sitting in uncomfortable SLA calls with Ashley Furniture without clean data.

**What we do**: Exist in the places David pays attention — carrier association newsletters, LinkedIn (logistics and ops circles), conference presence (NHFA, last-mile delivery events).

**Trigger to next stage**: A peer carrier mentions they're using Atlas, or David sees a case study showing FACR improvement.

---

### Stage 2: Aware / Problem Recognized

**David's state**: Recognizes the problem is real and that software might exist to address it. Starts paying attention.

**What he's doing**: Googling "delivery exception tracking software," asking peers at carrier events, maybe reading a LinkedIn post.

**What we do**: Content that maps to his search — problem framing content ("why your SLA conversations are hard"), not feature marketing. Landing page that speaks his language: FACR, SLA defense, retailer accountability.

**Trigger to next stage**: David shares a link with Maria, or reaches out for a demo.

---

### Stage 3: Consideration

**David's state**: Evaluating Atlas against doing nothing, continuing with spreadsheets, or looking at 1–2 alternatives.

**What he's doing**: Taking a demo, asking Maria to evaluate, reviewing pricing, checking references.

**Pain points in this stage**:
- "How do I know this will actually improve our numbers?"
- "How hard is it to get our team to adopt this?"
- "What does migration look like?"

**What we do**:
- Demo led by founder — show Morning Brief, exception workflow, FACR trend
- ROI calculator: enter current exception rate → see dollar cost → see savings at 88% FACR
- Reference call with a current customer in similar operation size
- Trial option: 30-day free trial for 1 warehouse, no credit card

**Trigger to next stage**: David asks Maria "do you want to try this?" and Maria says yes.

---

### Stage 4: Decision

**David's state**: Ready to buy, negotiating terms, getting internal approval.

**What he's doing**: Reviewing contract, getting IT/legal sign-off (usually minimal for SMB), deciding on tier.

**Friction points**:
- Annual vs monthly (we prefer annual)
- Multi-warehouse pricing (needs to see value before committing to Scale tier)
- IT asking about data security / PII handling

**What we do**:
- Standard contract with clear data handling terms
- Onboarding timeline committed upfront (14-day target)
- Champion David with "here's what the first 30 days looks like" doc

**Trigger to next stage**: Contract signed.

---

### Stage 5: Onboarding

**David's state**: Contract signed; now it's Maria's problem.

**What Maria needs**: Get up and running without losing a day of ops.

**Onboarding sequence**:
- Day 1: Account setup, first warehouse configured, Maria and dispatch team added
- Day 2–3: Exception reason codes customized, driver accounts created
- Day 3–5: Drivers download/bookmark mobile app, test exception log flow
- Day 7: First Morning Brief reviewed live with Maria on a call
- Day 14: First week of exception data reviewed — any gaps in logging?

**Success signal**: By Day 14, 80%+ of exceptions are being logged in Atlas (not just remembered).

---

### Stage 6: Adoption

**Maria's state**: Using Atlas daily. Seeing value accumulate.

**Critical moments**:
- First time the Morning Brief flags a stop that would have failed (and it doesn't)
- First time Maria shows David a FACR trend and he says "finally"
- First coaching conversation with a driver backed by FACR data

**Risk in this stage**: Maria adopts, but Marcus and the drivers don't log exceptions consistently. Data quality degrades. Analytics are useless.

**What we do**: Proactive CSM check-in at Day 30 reviewing exception log completeness. If < 80%, work with Maria to identify friction points.

---

### Stage 7: Expansion / Renewal

**David's state**: Sees FACR data from 90+ days of use. Has a case study for the Ashley call.

**Expansion triggers**:
- Adding a second warehouse (upgrade to Scale tier)
- Adding drivers who weren't in the initial rollout
- VP at another carrier in their network asks about the tool

**Renewal risk**: If FACR hasn't moved in 6 months, renewal is uncertain. The product must show impact, not just adoption.

**What we do**: QBR (Quarterly Business Review) with David — show FACR trend, exception recurrence rate drop, SLA scorecard. Make the ROI undeniable before renewal conversation.

## Journey 2 — Maria's Daily Use Journey

### 5:45am — Pre-Route (Morning)

**Maria arrives at warehouse.**

| Without Atlas | With Atlas |
|--------------|------------|
| Checks TMS for today's routes | Opens Atlas Morning Brief |
| Texts drivers about customer confirmations | Sees unconfirmed stops count at a glance |
| Calls any "at-risk" stops from memory | Taps unconfirmed list → calls from there |
| Checks Samsara for vehicle status | Sees vehicle alerts in Brief |
| Knows nothing about route risk | Sees high-risk route flags |
| All of this takes 30–45 min | All of this takes < 10 min |

**Key moment**: The first morning Maria opens the Brief and finds a flagged unconfirmed stop that she then confirms — and that stop delivers successfully — is the moment she becomes a champion.

---

### 6:00–8:00am — Routes Depart

**Maria monitors departures.**

| Without Atlas | With Atlas |
|--------------|------------|
| Routes leave and she hopes for the best | Sees confirmation status update as drivers check in |
| Calls drivers to check on risky stops | Gets push notification when exception is logged |
| Exception calls come in to her phone | Exception appears in queue with structured reason code |
| Reschedule is manual: call customer, update TMS | Tap reschedule trigger → customer notification auto-sent |

---

### 8:00am–3:00pm — Active Delivery Window

**Maria manages exceptions reactively.**

| Without Atlas | With Atlas |
|--------------|------------|
| Exception comes in via driver call | Real-time exception notification |
| Maria manually logs in TMS (maybe) | Exception already logged by driver |
| No visibility into pattern | Exception queue shows if same reason repeating |
| Retailer calls asking about failed delivery | Maria can pull exception detail instantly |

---

### 3:00–5:00pm — End of Day

**Maria closes out the day.**

| Without Atlas | With Atlas |
|--------------|------------|
| Manually enters exception notes into TMS | All exceptions already logged in structured form |
| David asks about today's numbers — she estimates | FACR for today visible in dashboard |
| Shift handoff is verbal | Exception queue shows open items for afternoon lead |

## Journey 3 — Marcus's Use Journey (Driver)

### 7:00am — Pre-Departure

**Marcus loads the truck.**

| Without Atlas | With Atlas |
|--------------|------------|
| Reviews PDF manifest on phone | Opens confirmation checklist in mobile app |
| Notes are incomplete or missing | Access notes, elevator status, customer confirmation visible |
| Finds out about elevator outage mid-route from another driver | Prior exception flag on the problematic stop |

---

### Mid-Route — Delivery Failure

**Stop 4: customer not home.**

| Without Atlas | With Atlas |
|--------------|------------|
| Calls dispatch to report | Opens exception logging flow on phone |
| Dispatch asks questions to understand the situation | Selects reason code, adds notes, submits in < 60 seconds |
| Exception logged manually by Maria hours later | Exception in queue immediately; reschedule triggered |
| Marcus has to fill out written exception form at EOD | Done. No EOD paperwork. |

---

### End of Week — Performance Check

**Marcus wants to know how he did.**

| Without Atlas | With Atlas |
|--------------|------------|
| No visibility unless Maria tells him | Opens self-view in mobile app |
| Doesn't know how he compares to team | Sees FACR: 94% (2nd on team) |
| Coaching conversation is based on Maria's memory | His exception history is right there |

## Moments That Make or Break Adoption

| Moment | Risk | Success Signal |
|--------|------|---------------|
| Maria's first Morning Brief | Brief loads slowly or has wrong data → she doesn't come back | Brief loads < 2s; data is accurate; she tells someone about it |
| First driver exception log | Too many taps, app crashes, driver gives up → data gap | First exception logged in < 60s with reason code |
| First FACR trend view after 30 days | Chart is empty or has obvious errors → trust broken | Trend line matches what Maria remembers happening |
| David's first partner report | Data doesn't match what David knows to be true → escalation | Report passes sanity check; David uses it in real meeting |
| 90-day FACR review | No improvement visible → renewal at risk | FACR is visibly trending up; recurrence rate is down |
