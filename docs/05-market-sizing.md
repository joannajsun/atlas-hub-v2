## TAM — Total Addressable Market

### Top-Down

The global last-mile delivery software market was valued at approximately **$3.5B in 2023** and is projected to reach **$8.6B by 2028** (CAGR ~20–22%), driven by:
- E-commerce volume growth: US e-commerce sales surpassed $1.1T in 2023
- Rising consumer expectations: same-day/next-day delivery now expected across categories
- Labor cost pressures: operators need to do more with the same headcount
- Big-and-bulky e-commerce growth: home furnishings online sales grew 34% since 2021

*Sources: Grand View Research, Allied Market Research, US Census Bureau e-commerce reports*

**TAM: $8.6B (2028)**

## SAM — Serviceable Addressable Market

Atlas Hub 2.0 is purpose-built for the white-glove / big-and-bulky delivery segment in the United States. This is a distinct segment from food delivery, parcel, and freight — different economics, different tools, different problems.

### Segment Sizing

**US white-glove delivery market**: ~$15B in total delivery revenue (furniture, appliance, fitness equipment)
- Approximately 2,800 regional carriers operating in this space
- Average carrier: 20–80 trucks, 3–5 retail partner contracts
- Typical tech spend: $800–$2,500/month on TMS + telematics + route tools

**Software addressable within this segment**:
- Assume 40% of carriers are in the digitization window (not using enterprise TMS, open to modern tools): ~1,120 carriers
- Average ACV target: $12,000–$18,000/year (~$1,000–$1,500/month for 20–50 truck operation)
- Mid-point: $15,000 ACV × 1,120 carriers = **$16.8M ARR at full penetration**

Adjusting for a more conservative 25% addressable penetration in a crowded market:
**SAM: ~$420M** (accounts for the full software stack, not just Atlas's share)

## Market Dynamics

### Tailwinds
1. **E-commerce furniture/appliance growth**: Online big-and-bulky sales grew 34% 2021–2024 and continue rising
2. **Retailer SLA pressure**: Major retailers (Ashley, Best Buy, Wayfair) are actively scoring and switching carrier partners based on delivery performance data — creating urgency for carriers to instrument their ops
3. **Labor cost pressure**: Average delivery driver wage up 18% since 2022 — every failed delivery is more expensive
4. **Digitization gap**: Most regional carriers still use legacy TMS + spreadsheets — fertile ground for modern tooling

### Headwinds
1. **Sales cycle length**: Ops software to small carriers is relationship-dependent, can take 2–4 months to close
2. **Integration resistance**: Carriers worry about disrupting existing TMS workflows
3. **Incumbent habit**: Spreadsheets and text threads are "free" — switching cost is behavioral, not just financial
4. **Bringg/enterprise push down**: Well-funded players may extend downmarket if the opportunity is validated

### Why the Timing Is Right
The combination of growing delivery volume, rising failure costs, and retailer accountability pressure has created a moment where regional carriers are actively looking for better tooling. The window for a purpose-built solution is open — before enterprise players push downmarket or a well-funded startup captures the category.
